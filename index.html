The frame

A working split for what an "AI problem" usually is.

We work with companies on a simple framing. Roughly 60% of the work that gets labeled "an AI problem" is really traditional code and database work. About 30% is rule-based logic, state machines, and decisions that do not need a language model. Only about 10% is a genuine AI problem where a model earns its keep.

60.
Traditional code

Payroll-tax dispute triage is mostly rule-based logic and a clean retrieval layer over policy, not a chatbot.

30.
Rule-based logic

State machines and structured decisions across intake, compliance lookup, and payroll-dispute routing.

10.
Genuine AI work

Sales enablement and account-specific prep is where models actually earn their seat.

Applied to a company like Justworks, that framing usually lands in three places. Payroll-tax dispute triage is mostly rule-based logic and a clean retrieval layer over policy, not a chatbot. Sales enablement and account-specific prep is where models actually earn their seat. Engineering workflow itself is a governance question more than a capability question, because the tools are already in the building.

The part most companies miss is that the training data doubles as a specification for the orchestration work that follows. What a support agent actually asks the model, what they ignore, what they correct: that becomes the spec for the system that fixes the root cause.
